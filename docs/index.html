Motivation

Maps and geographic information systems (GIS) have become crucial communication, decision-making, and outreach tools for local decision-makers. Their ability to generate and disseminate knowledge have improved through the advancements in interactivity and data sharing. On the other hand, the worldwide interest in the market and technologies related to solar photovoltaic (PV) energy is increasing mostly due the increasing demand for sustainable energy production and energy security, which are aggravated by issues like climate change and environmental problems associated with the development of conventional energy resources (Fraunhofer ISE 2015). Thus, advancements to photovoltaic (PV) technologies are re-configuring stakeholder relations and bringing new social and technical challenges and opportunities to cities. As these technologies become easier to install, utilities, willingly or not, need to adapt their infrastructure and operation practices to accommodate these new forms and sites of generation. (Fish, C. S., & Calvert, K. 2017). Interactive web maps have the potential to facilitate and perhaps coordinate informed decisions within and between these stakeholder groups. Indeed, urban-scale energy resource mapping has become increasingly common, marked by the rise in online energy maps in cities across the world (Kanters et al. 2014)

August 11, 2021 Solar PV rooftop estimation project

Description

This project aims to develop a mobile app that estimates the potential solar energy of the selected building rooftop and generates a report based on the calculation. It serves as project management tool for installers to carry out feasibility studies, site surveys, designing and prepare techno-commercial proposals for their customers while providing feedbacks to way end customers about their approximate cost for solar panel installation, applicable government incentives, finance, and information about nearby Solar Australian PV Rooftop Installer.

This app has a default map that allows users to assess solar potential. Wider implementation of solar energy systems can be achieved by implementing the function to not only view general solar potential in a location, but to calculate solar potential across a specific rooftop which may help with decision making. Users can click on any building to compare solar potential, pan and zoom around the map and assess solar potential for every structure represented on the map. It is especially helpful as opposed to those that required the user to know a specific address. This benefits solar energy developers who are interested in identifying clients’ buildings with ideal solar potential, or a utility system operator interested in identifying rooftops within a specified distance of a node on the electricity grid.

Additionally, the map allows users to draw on a rooftop to assess solar potential for an isolated area of the building which enables a homeowner to assess potential on only the part of the roof where they may want to install a PV system. Clicking on the buildings on the map allows users to analyze all aspects of system potential (cost, savings, energy production, and environmental benefits). It is important as prospective investors (homeowners and third-party developers) are more motivated to deploy PV systems based on both economic and environmental benefits if site-specific information about the multiple benefits of solar are provided. In addition, cost will be the most important decision criterion for some users, while for others, savings will be the motivating factor. Another advantage of this grouping was the display of options to connect with an installer. Map users can realize the economic and environmental benefits of a system at their location of interest more easily by connecting them directly with a solar installation company or offering a list of companies. The map also allows the user to generate a report that helps in connecting users and installers. This provides information about all aspects of system potential, benefits of solar, costs, and solar installation services to users who seriously about installing a PV system which is ideal for allowing users to make the most informed decision possible.

Several map interaction operators were widely used in the map of, including search, retrieve, pan, zoom, import, and calculate. The search operator likewise important because it allowed the user to easily find their home within the map without panning and zooming. This is necessary if the goal is to allow users to assess the energy potential of a particular building. The retrieve operator afforded users the ability to query the map for more information. This is crucial for users who want to assess solar potential and information about currently installed solar systems. The pan and zoom operators, permits users to move around the subject area. Finally, an calculate operator is automatically executed in every map at loading time as it performs computations of built-in algorithm in order to generate useful information based on current statistics collected.
